The Basics of Financial Literacy: Saving and Investing

Financial freedom takes time, patience, and just a little bit of know-how. Shondaland is here to help with our ongoing series on all-things money. This installment: when, how, and where to save and invest.

A CREATIVE COLLABORATION WITH SHONDALAND AND MGO CPAs & ADVISORS
Oct 28, 2019
Jamie GrillGetty Images

Cardi B said it best: “All I really wanna see is the money.” Well, while seeing the money is great, what comes after you start to pad out your bank account is just as important as how you got the coins there in the first place. Thus, Shondaland, along with the help and expertise of MGO CPAs & Advisors accounting firm, is launching our financial education series, with the goal of helping anyone and everyone win at getting to your financial “happily ever after.” Designed to help you understand both what's influencing and driving our economy and also the fundamentals of basic fiscal planning, this information is designed to help you take control and effectively manage your finances now and as you plan for the future. You work hard, so, from budgeting to saving to investing, it’s all about making your money work hard for you, too.

Everything You Need to Know About Diversifying

When you were a kid, there’s a good chance that you had a cute little piggy bank sitting right on your bedroom dresser. You were probably encouraged to put change, and sometimes even a dollar bill or two, into your bank to teach you how to save. And you probably loved it! As a small child, there was nothing more exciting than realizing you had five whole dollars that were all yours.

As an adult, saving is much less exciting. When we get a paycheck, most of us want to go out and spend it like it’s the last night of the world. But consider this: seven out of 10 Millennials believe being able to live month to month as being financially stable, and more than half admit to still getting financial help from their parents. What's more, most financial advisers suggest that retirees (those 65 years and older) have anywhere between $1 million and $1.5 million set aside for retirement — and yet, according to a 2018 study by Northwestern Mutual, 21 percent of Americans have no retirement savings, while 10 percent have less than $5,000. When it comes to retiring Baby Boomers, only a third have between nothing and $25,000 set aside, and for the U.S. millennial population — which, at 71 million and counting, will soon surge past the Boomers — nearly six in 10 have no savings whatsoever.

Amber Hawkins

Sounds bleak, right? Maybe. But, the truth is, we all have futures to plan for. And while that might seem like a long uphill climb, rest assured, saving as an adult starts in much the same way as it did with your piggy bank: start small, build your nest egg, and then, when you're comfortable, start investing so as to grow your money even more. Proper saving and investing takes time, patience, and a little bit of know-how, so here’s everything you need to know to being doing that, now.

Starting your savings

The piggy bank approach

When it comes to socking away money, you can actually use a real piggy bank — or a shoebox, or whatever you feel comfortable with — to establish the habit of saving. To start, empty your pocket change at the end of the day then, when it's built up, take it to your bank and put it directly into your savings account.

Another manual savings strategy? Put away 10 bucks a week. That’s nothing right? Well, if you put in 10 dollars a week, you’ll have over 500 dollars at the end of a year. It adds up fast! It might seem like a silly way to start, but this can help you learn how to live off less than you are actually making, which is the cornerstone of establishing saving habits.


The 50/30/20 plan

While saving cash works, realistically, it’s probably easier for most people to open an electronic savings account. That way you always know how much money is in the account at any given time. A good strategy for starting your savings — which comes from establishing a budget — is establishing a 50/30/20 spending plan: 50 percent of your after-tax income goes to needs (groceries, rent, utilities, transportation, insurance, etc.) 30 percent goes to wants (gym memberships, dining out, shopping) and 20 percent goes into savings.

Savings apps

Depending on where you are in your earnings, 20% might feel like a lot to take out of your paycheck for savings. If so, start with just 5% of your earnings and try to work your way up to 20%. In order to help, set up automatic transfers for each pay period so you don’t even having to think about saving, it’s just happening automatically. Other automated tools like the apps Chime, Digit or Qapital all automatically transfer small amounts from your checking account to a separate savings account, and even larger institutions like Bank of America have services that move small increments of money to your savings every time you swipe your debit card.

Taking advantage of an employee retirement plan

Company-offered retirement plans, or a 401K, are a powerful savings tool that, should your specific employer offer it, you should strongly consider investing in. Here are the basics:

  • A 401k is a defined-pension account in which contributions are deducted from an employee’s paycheck and placed in a savings account.
  • These funds are tax-deferred until withdrawn after retirement or otherwise permitted by applicable law.
  • As of 2019, deductions are limited to a maximum pre-tax annual contribution of $19,000.

    Almost $20,000 may seem like a lot to take out your annual salary, especially if you are in a certain earning bracket. But, like setting up a basic savings, you can start small with your own retirement plan. With a one percent contribution to a retirement plan, for instance, you aren’t likely to notice much of a difference in your paycheck. Once you’ve adjusted to living with your initial percentage contribution, then, also like your savings plan, you increase it whenever you feel comfortable. A smart starter system is to raise your contribution one percent every six months as a test to make sure you can live happily with that amount of money taken out of your living wage.

    If your company doesn’t have a plan in place or one that you don’t feel happy with, always follow the simple rule of saving 10 percent of your income each pay period towards your future retirement. If you start contributing or saving later in life, you’ll probably need to set aside more than 10 percent. If so, consult your budget and figure out what number works best for you.

    Learning the language of advanced investing

    Now that we’ve covered basic savings, let’s get into the nitty gritty of stocks, bonds, and other investment vehicles that can vastly increase your auxiliary income.

    Investing can be confusing. It can feel like a vast landscape. It seems infinitely easy to make cataclysmic mistakes. We get it! The fear that an honest misstep might rob you of your ultimate goal — to accumulate money for your future — is enough to hold you back from dipping your toe into the investment pool. But, while it’s no bad thing to be cautious, basic investing is something anyone can grasp.

    The first step is to learn and understand terms and concepts you’ll commonly hear once you begin the investment process. Here are the most common types of investment you’re likely to encounter:

    • Stocks — One of the oldest forms of investing. Purchasing a stock means that you have bought ownership (or shares) of a company. As long as the company makes money and grows, your stock’s value will too.
      • Bonds — Another fairly common way to get a return on money. In this case, you’re loaning to a company, government, or other entity to help it grow. In other words, by buying bonds, you are lending money to the company or institution that issued it. As the business thrives you earn a return on your investment.
        • Real Estate Investing — If you own property, you have a real estate investment. You can own property that you live in, or properties that you rent to others. You can also buy into a real estate investment trust, which is like owning stock, but your money gives you shares in an actual location and property.
          • Mutual Funds — Mutual Funds are a way of investing in companies through a 401(k) or Roth IRA. Mutual Funds are fairly easy to understand and track, but they do have fees which can eat into the profit you are making and they can also boost your tax bill at the end of the year.

            Once you’ve got the basics down, it’s time to speak to a financial advisor who can walk you through all of the above and how these concepts can help you grow a nice, healthy portfolio of investments that will, hopefully, yield returns that can either go toward retirement, savings, or, as long its responsible, a big purchase your life might require.

            Investing in an unstable economy

            With so much turmoil going on with tariffs, taxes, and, according to some, a looming recession, our economy goes up and down a lot. A. LOT. A myriad of factors can affect the health of the economy, and, despite our best research, there’s no way to know when it might take a dip or have a huge jump.

            In any case, your best tactic when things head south is to keep a cool and level head with your investments. While fear can make you want to immediately jump ship and sell off what you have, best practices mandate avoiding unloading or liquidating investments because you stand to lose money in the short term. Nearly all the investment vehicles we’ve discussed are long plays and, if you’ve done research and made sound investments, they should grow over time. Indeed, sometimes things are darkest before the dawn, and your investments certainly might take a knocking before they start making money for you again. But, most often, the best plan is to stay the course, ride out the storm, and hold on until the downturn ends.
